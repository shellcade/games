hard	The transistor, invented at Bell Labs in 1947 by Bardeen, Brattain, and Shockley, replaced the bulky, fragile vacuum tube and launched the digital age. By switching and amplifying electrical signals, transistors form the basic building blocks of all modern computers. Following Moore's Law, the number packed onto a single chip roughly doubled every two years for decades, with today's processors holding billions in an area smaller than a fingernail.
hard	The water cycle moves an estimated 121,000 cubic kilometers of water over land each year through evaporation, condensation, and precipitation. Solar energy lifts water vapor from oceans and lakes; it cools and forms clouds, then falls as rain or snow. Roughly 60% of land precipitation evaporates again, while the rest feeds rivers and groundwater. This endless circulation regulates climate and sustains nearly every ecosystem on the planet.
hard	The Library of Alexandria, founded in the 3rd century BCE under the Ptolemaic dynasty, aimed to collect all the knowledge of the known world. At its height it may have held hundreds of thousands of scrolls, drawing scholars from across the Mediterranean. Its gradual decline, hastened by fire, neglect, and political turmoil, robbed history of countless lost works, a loss that still haunts students of the ancient world today.
hard	An object in orbit is, in a sense, perpetually falling toward the body it circles, yet moving sideways fast enough to keep missing it. The International Space Station, orbiting at about 250 miles up, travels at roughly 17,500 miles per hour, completing one lap of the Earth every 90 minutes or so. Its crew therefore witnesses 16 sunrises and sunsets each day, a dizzying schedule that complicates any normal sense of time.
hard	The Mariana Trench plunges to about 36,070 feet (10,994 meters) below sea level at a point called Challenger Deep, deeper than Mount Everest is tall. The pressure there exceeds 1,000 times that at the surface, enough to crush most submarines. Yet life persists even in this crushing darkness: strange fish, amphipods, and microbes thrive, reminding us how little of our own ocean we have truly explored or understood.
hard	In economics, the concept of "compound interest" describes how money grows when earnings are reinvested to generate further earnings. At an annual rate of 7%, an investment doubles roughly every 10 years, following the handy "Rule of 72" (divide 72 by the rate). Over long periods the effect is dramatic: a single dollar invested at 7% would grow to about 868 dollars after 100 years, illustrating the quiet, relentless power of time.

By comparing the readable Greek text with the unknown symbols above, the scholar Jean-Francois Champollion finally cracked the code in 1822, unlocking thousands of years of silent Egyptian history.
